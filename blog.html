Complete Guide to MIDI Drum Mapping Between Popular Libraries

Published: May 26, 2025

MIDI drum mapping is essential for musicians and producers who work with different drum libraries or virtual instruments. This guide will explore common mapping techniques, compare popular drum libraries, and show you how to efficiently convert between different formats.

Understanding MIDI Drum Maps

MIDI drum maps define which MIDI note numbers correspond to specific drum sounds. The General MIDI (GM) standard assigns specific note numbers to drum sounds (e.g., note 36 for kick drum, 38 for snare), but many virtual instruments and drum libraries use their own proprietary mappings.

This inconsistency creates challenges when you want to:

  • Transfer drum patterns between different software instruments
  • Use MIDI grooves with a different drum library than they were created for
  • Collaborate with others who use different drum libraries

Best Practices for MIDI Drum Mapping

  • Create templates for frequently used conversions
  • Document your mappings to maintain consistency across projects
  • Test thoroughly after conversion to ensure all articulations translate properly
  • Consider velocity mapping alongside note mapping, as different libraries respond differently to velocity values
